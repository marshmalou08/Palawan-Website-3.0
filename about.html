The Philippines' Crown Jewel

Palawan is a long, narrow island province in the southwestern Philippines, stretching 450 km between the South China Sea and the Sulu Sea. It is the largest province in the country by land area and home to one of the world's most diverse ecosystems.

The province encompasses the main island of Palawan and 1,780 smaller islands and islets, forming a rich mosaic of coral reefs, rainforests, mangroves, and mountain ranges that have earned it multiple international conservation designations.

  • Location: Southwestern Philippines, MIMAROPA region
  • Capital: Puerto Princesa (a highly urbanized city)
  • Area: 14,649 km² (largest province in the Philippines)
  • Population: Approximately 939,000
  • Language: Filipino, English, Cuyonon, Tagbanua, Palawano

Short History

From ancient seafarers to Spanish colonizers, Palawan's history is as rich as its landscapes.

Ancient Roots

Archaeological finds in Tabon Cave on Quezon Island date human habitation back 47,000 years, making Palawan one of the oldest inhabited places in Southeast Asia.

Sultanate Era

Before Spanish colonization, the Sultanate of Brunei held significant influence over northern Palawan, and trade networks connected the island to China, Borneo, and Java.

Spanish Period

Spanish colonizers built Fort Santa Isabel in Taytay in 1667. Taytay served as Palawan's capital before Puerto Princesa took over in the 19th century.

Modern Era

Puerto Princesa became a city in 1970. Palawan's tourism boom accelerated after the Underground River earned UNESCO and New 7 Wonders recognition.

Culture & Traditions

Palawan is home to multiple indigenous groups preserving ancient traditions.

Indigenous Peoples

The three principal indigenous groups of Palawan — the Batak, Tagbanua, and Palaw'an (Palawano) — have inhabited the island for thousands of years. Each group maintains distinct traditions, languages, spiritual practices, and relationships with the land and sea.

The Tagbanua, recognized as one of the oldest ethnic groups in the Philippines, practice a form of animism centered on ancestral spirits and forest guardians. Their traditional fishing techniques and forest-gathering practices are still alive today.

  • The Batak are known for their honey-gathering and forest foraging traditions
  • The Tagbanua maintain ancient burial rituals and oral histories
  • The Palaw'an are skilled craftspeople producing unique woven goods
  • All groups practice a deep reverence for the natural environment

Why Visit Palawan?

World-class recognition, natural wonders, and unmatched beauty.

Award-Winning Beauty

Palawan has been voted the world's best island multiple times by Condé Nast Traveler and Travel + Leisure readers.

Unrivalled Marine Life

Two UNESCO World Heritage Sites — the Underground River and Tubbataha Reef — plus some of the richest coral reefs in the Coral Triangle.

Unique Wildlife

The Palawan bearcat, Palawan peacock-pheasant, and Philippine mouse deer are found only here — a naturalist's paradise.
